2.2 Values Integration and Decision-Making
The SHE IS AI Decision Framework
Every significant organizational decision passes through our four-value filter:
• Ethics Check: Does this decision serve human dignity and community benefit? Will it advance ethical AI development or create potential for harm?
• Equity Assessment: Who benefits from this decision? What barriers might it create or remove? How does it affect underrepresented communities?
6
• Inclusion Evaluation: Whose voices informed this decision? Are we creating authentic participation or superficial representation?
• Empowerment Analysis: Does this decision increase community agency and self-determination? Will it build capacity for independent innovation and leadership?
• Creative Innovation Review: How does this decision support artistic expression and imaginative problem-solving? Does it honor diverse cultural approaches to creativity and innovation?
• Sustainability Assessment: What are the long-term environmental and community impacts? Does this decision strengthen rather than deplete resources and relationships?
• Transparency Evaluation: Are we being open and honest about this decision's implications? Can community members understand and question our reasoning?
• Accountability Review: How will we measure the impact of this decision? What mechanisms exist for course correction if outcomes don't align with intentions?

• Ethics guides our equity work by ensuring opportunities create systems that serve human dignity.
• Equity informs our inclusion practices by helping us understand different groups need different supports.

Ethical & Responsible AI Frameworks
3.1 Ethical AI vs. Responsible AI
At SHE IS AI, we understand that preparing women to be leading AI Xperts requires deep knowledge of both moral foundations and practical implementation strategies for AI development. As we create educational content and teach AI concepts to position one million women as leading AI Xperts worldwide, we ensure our community understands the critical distinction between ethical and responsible AI approaches, recognizing that both perspectives are essential for those who will influence, evaluate, and guide AI development in their careers and organizations.
Definitions
Ethical AI focuses on the moral principles and values that should guide artificial intelligence development. It asks fundamental questions about right and wrong in AI systems, examining whether these technologies align with human values like fairness, autonomy, and dignity. Ethical AI is primarily concerned with the philosophical foundations of what makes AI morally acceptable and serves as the moral compass for determining what we should build.
Responsible AI takes a more practical approach, emphasizing the implementation of processes, governance structures, and accountability mechanisms throughout the AI lifecycle. It focuses on translating ethical principles into concrete practices that organizations can follow to ensure their AI systems operate safely and beneficially in real-world contexts. Responsible AI provides an operational roadmap for how to build ethical AI systems safely and accountably.
Together, these approaches create the comprehensive framework that guides our educational content development and ensures that the AI Xperts we develop understand both the moral imperatives and practical requirements for evaluating, influencing, and advocating for technology that empowers rather than exploits communities worldwide.
Elements of Ethical AI
Fairness and Non-Discrimination form the cornerstone of ethical AI, requiring systems to treat all individuals and groups equitably without perpetuating bias or prejudice. At SHE IS AI, we teach that fairness must be designed into AI systems from the beginning rather than retrofitted after problems emerge, and we prepare our community to advocate for diverse perspectives in determining what fairness means across different cultural contexts and community needs.
Transparency and Explainability demand that AI decision-making processes remain interpretable and explainable to those affected by them. We educate our community that transparency goes beyond technical documentation to include clear communication about AI capabilities, limitations, and potential impacts in language that communities can understand and use to make informed decisions about AI adoption and usage.
32
Privacy Protection ensures that AI systems respect individual data rights and maintain confidentiality while honoring different cultural approaches to privacy and information sharing. Our curriculum recognizes that privacy expectations vary across global communities and that robust protection requires both technical safeguards and community consultation about data usage and sharing practices.
Human Autonomy Preservation guarantees that AI augments rather than replaces human decision-making in critical areas, maintaining human agency and community self-determination. We teach that effective AI systems enhance human capabilities and community wisdom rather than substituting algorithmic decisions for human judgment, particularly in areas affecting individual lives and community wellbeing.
Beneficence and Non-Maleficence require that AI systems actively promote human welfare and minimize potential harm, prioritizing community benefit over commercial gain or technological advancement for its own sake. Our educational content emphasizes that AI development should serve authentic community needs and contribute to social justice rather than perpetuating existing inequalities or creating new forms of harm.
Elements of Responsible AI
Governance Frameworks establish clear policies, procedures, and oversight mechanisms for AI application and guidance that include diverse voices in decision-making processes. At SHE IS AI, we teach that effective governance requires authentic community participation rather than token consultation, and we prepare our community to advocate for systems where those most affected by AI have genuine authority over application and implementation decisions.
Risk Assessment and Management processes identify potential negative impacts and implement mitigation strategies throughout the AI lifecycle, with particular attention to how risks may disproportionately affect marginalized communities. We educate our community in comprehensive risk evaluation that considers not just technical failures but also social, cultural, and economic impacts that may harm vulnerable populations or exacerbate existing inequalities.
Accountability Structures define who is responsible for AI system outcomes and establish clear chains of responsibility that include community accountability mechanisms alongside organizational oversight. Our curriculum emphasizes that accountability requires transparent reporting, accessible grievance processes, and meaningful consequences when AI systems cause harm to individuals or communities.
Continuous Monitoring and Auditing ensure that AI systems perform as intended and identify issues before they cause harm, with ongoing community feedback integration and responsive improvement protocols. We teach that monitoring must include community voices and cultural competency assessment rather than relying solely on technical metrics that may miss important social impacts.
Stakeholder Engagement brings diverse perspectives into AI development, including affected communities, domain experts, and regulatory bodies, with particular emphasis on centering
33
voices that have been historically excluded from technology development. Our educational content teaches authentic engagement approaches that honor community expertise and ensure that consultation translates into real influence over AI system design and implementation.
Documentation and Compliance practices maintain detailed records of AI system design, testing, and performance for audit and regulatory purposes while ensuring transparency that enables community understanding and oversight. We teach that documentation should serve community empowerment and accountability rather than merely satisfying regulatory requirements.
Why Both Are Needed
The distinction between ethical and responsible AI matters because both perspectives are necessary for creating trustworthy AI systems that serve diverse communities effectively. Ethical AI provides the moral compass that guides what we should build, ensuring that AI development serves human dignity and community empowerment. Indirect Communication (Japan, Thailand, many African nations): Emphasizes context, nonverbal cues, and subtle language to maintain harmony and respect

Achievement and Recognition

Collectivistic Cultures (China, Guatemala, Philippines): Prioritize group harmony, consensus-building, and shared success as foundational values
We design recognition systems that celebrate both personal achievement and group success.
Time and Process Orientation
Linear Time (Germany, Switzerland, US): Views time as structured with clear beginnings and endings, emphasizing schedules and milestone-driven progress
Cyclical Time (many Indigenous communities, some African and Asian societies): Views time as circular with repeating patterns, emphasizing natural cycles and relationship-building
We respect both approaches through flexible scheduling and structured plus iterative learning options.

Glossary
At SHE IS AI, we recognize that shared understanding begins with common language. This glossary provides definitions of key diversity, equity, inclusion, and AI ethics terms within our global context, acknowledging that terminology evolves over time and varies across cultures. These definitions serve as starting points for building understanding across our diverse community.
Our commitment to multilingual accessibility goes beyond translation to cultural adaptation and ongoing learning about how equity concepts are understood across different contexts. This glossary evolves with feedback from our global community as we work together to shape the future of AI worldwide.
Accessibility: The design of products, services, environments, and experiences to be usable by people with the widest range of abilities, disabilities, and circumstances. In AI education, accessibility ensures that learning materials work effectively with assistive technologies, include multiple format options, and accommodate different abilities and technological access levels.
Affinity Groups: Employee or community resource groups organized around shared identities, experiences, or interests that provide support, professional development, and advocacy for members while contributing to organizational goals.
AI Governance: The frameworks, policies, and processes used to guide responsible AI development, deployment, and oversight, ensuring AI systems serve societal benefit while minimizing potential harms through inclusive decision-making and accountability mechanisms.
Algorithmic Bias: Systematic and unfair discrimination embedded in algorithmic systems that disadvantages certain groups based on race, gender, age, disability, or other characteristics. This bias can occur in training data, model design, or implementation processes, leading to AI systems that perpetuate or amplify existing social inequalities and create disparate outcomes for different communities.
Allyship: The ongoing process of building relationships based on trust, consistency, and accountability with marginalized individuals and groups. Allyship involves acknowledging oppression and actively committing to reducing one's own complicity while strengthening knowledge and awareness.
Alternative Learning Paths: Educational and skill development routes that exist outside traditional formal academic systems, including self-directed learning, online courses, bootcamps, apprenticeships, vocational training, community-based education, and experiential
2
learning. These pathways recognize that expertise and knowledge can be acquired through diverse methods beyond conventional university degrees.
Anti-Racism: The policy or practice of opposing racism and promoting racial tolerance through institutional and policy changes, individual education, and community engagement that addresses systemic barriers.
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI): A theoretical form of AI that would possess human-level cognitive abilities across all domains, including reasoning, learning, creativity, and problem-solving. Unlike current AI systems that excel at specific tasks, AGI would demonstrate flexible intelligence comparable to human cognition, raising profound questions about consciousness, control, and societal impact that require diverse voices and ethical frameworks to address responsibly.
Belonging: The feeling of being an integral, valued member of a community where one can bring their full, authentic self and experience psychological safety, connection, and meaning. Belonging is the outcome when diversity, equity, and inclusion practices succeed.
Bias Interruption: The practice of recognizing and actively disrupting biased thinking patterns, discriminatory behaviors, and exclusionary practices in real-time within organizations and communities. Bias interruption requires developing awareness of unconscious biases and implementing strategies to create more equitable outcomes through immediate intervention and systemic change.
Bias Mitigation: The systematic process of identifying, reducing, and preventing unfair discrimination in AI systems through technical, procedural, and organizational interventions. This includes training in bias detection techniques, fairness metrics implementation, algorithmic auditing methods, and advocacy for AI systems that serve diverse communities equitably across different cultural and demographic contexts.
BIPOC: An acronym used to refer to Black, Indigenous, and People of Color, based on recognition of collective experiences of systemic racism. As with any identity term, it is up to individuals to use this term as an identifier.
Colonialism/Decolonization: Colonialism refers to the practice of political and economic control over another territory and its people, often accompanied by cultural suppression and resource extraction. Decolonization encompasses both the historical process of gaining political independence and ongoing efforts to challenge colonial mindsets, restore indigenous knowledge systems, and create more equitable relationships between communities and
3
institutions.
Community-Led: An approach where community members have authentic decision-making power, agenda-setting authority, and resource allocation control over initiatives that affect them. Community-led work recognizes that those most impacted by challenges are best positioned to identify solutions and should have primary authority over addressing their circumstances.
Cultural Appropriation: The adoption or use of elements from one culture by members of a different cultural group, particularly when a dominant culture takes from a marginalized culture without permission, understanding, or credit, often resulting in misrepresentation or exploitation.
Cultural Competency: A process of embracing diversity and learning about people from other cultural backgrounds. The key element is respect for the ways that others live in and organize the world and an openness to learn from them.
Cultural Intelligence: The capability to function effectively in culturally diverse settings, including awareness of cultural differences, knowledge of cultural systems, skills for bridging cultural gaps, and motivation to engage across cultures.
Data Sovereignty: The concept that communities and individuals should have control over how their data is collected, stored, analyzed, and used, particularly regarding cultural knowledge, community information, and personal data. Data sovereignty recognizes that data governance is fundamentally about power, self-determination, and protecting community interests from exploitation.
Digital Divide: The gap between those who have access to modern information and communication technology and those who do not, encompassing disparities in internet connectivity, device access, digital literacy skills, and technological infrastructure. This divide often reflects and reinforces existing inequalities based on geography, economic status, age, education, and other factors.
Displaced Persons: Individuals who have been forced to leave their homes or places of habitual residence due to armed conflict, situations of generalized violence, violations of human rights, natural or human-made disasters, or other circumstances that have seriously disturbed public order. This includes internally displaced persons who remain within their country's borders as well as those who have crossed international boundaries.
Diversity: The presence and representation of different identities, perspectives, experiences, and ways of thinking within our AI ecosystem. Diversity encompasses visible and invisible

dimensions including race, ethnicity, gender, age, sexual orientation, disability, neurodiversity, socioeconomic background, geographic location, educational path, professional experience, and cognitive approaches to problem-solving.
Endangered Language: A language that is at risk of falling out of use as its speakers die out or shift to speaking other languages. These languages face declining numbers of native speakers and may eventually become extinct without preservation efforts. Endangered languages represent irreplaceable cultural heritage and unique ways of understanding and expressing human experience.
Equality: Treating everyone the same by providing identical resources, opportunities, and support regardless of their starting point or circumstances. Equality assumes that fair treatment means uniform treatment, offering the same tools and pathways to all individuals without accounting for different barriers or advantages they may face.
Equity: Creating pathways for every person to thrive in the AI revolution by ensuring fair treatment, access, and opportunity for all. Equity means recognizing where systemic disadvantages exist and boldly creating solutions that level the playing field, ensuring AI's transformative power reaches every community and uplifts every voice.
Ethics: The study of moral principles that govern behavior and decision-making, examining what is right, wrong, good, and harmful in human actions and their consequences for individuals and communities.
Ethical AI: The practice of understanding, applying, and advocating for AI systems according to moral principles that prioritize human welfare, fairness, transparency, and respect for human rights and dignity across all communities.
Explainable AI (XAI): AI systems designed to provide clear, understandable explanations of their decision-making processes, allowing humans to comprehend how and why specific outputs or recommendations were generated. XAI is essential for building trust, ensuring accountability, and enabling effective human oversight of AI systems, particularly in high-stakes applications.
Fairness in AI: The principle that AI systems should produce equitable outcomes and treatment across different demographic groups, avoiding discrimination and ensuring that benefits and risks are distributed justly. Fairness in AI requires ongoing assessment, diverse development teams, and community input to address both individual and group-level equity considerations.

Global South/Global North: Terms used to describe the geopolitical and economic division between wealthier, industrialized countries (Global North) and developing nations (Global South), recognizing that economic and political power dynamics affect access to technology, education, and opportunities. Grassroots Organizing: Community-driven advocacy and action that builds power from the bottom up, emphasizing local leadership, authentic community participation, and collective action to address systemic challenges. Grassroots organizing prioritizes community knowledge and self-determination over external expertise or top-down solutions.
Human-in-the-Loop: AI system design that maintains meaningful human control and oversight throughout the decision-making process, ensuring that human judgment, values, and accountability remain central to AI-driven outcomes. This approach recognizes that AI should augment rather than replace human decision-making, particularly in high-stakes situations.
Inclusion: Creating spaces where every voice is not just heard but celebrated, where diverse perspectives drive innovation, and where all individuals feel empowered to bring their authentic selves to shape AI's future. Inclusion means building environments where everyone feels valued, respected, and essential to the conversation, transforming diversity from simple representation into active participation and meaningful influence.
Inclusive Excellence: The recognition that a community or institution's success is dependent on how well it values, engages, and includes the rich diversity of its constituents.
Inclusive Language: Language that includes all persons in its references and avoids assumptions about identity, experience, or circumstances. Inclusive language recognizes the diversity of experiences and identities.
Indigenous Knowledge: Traditional knowledge systems, practices, and worldviews of indigenous peoples that inform understanding and problem-solving approaches based on centuries of accumulated wisdom about sustainable relationships with land, community, and
6
natural systems. This knowledge encompasses unique ways of understanding the world that can inform ethical AI development and holistic approaches to technology innovation.
Intersectional Feminism: A framework that recognizes how gender intersects with race, class, sexuality, ability, nationality, and other identities to create unique experiences of oppression and privilege. Intersectional feminism addresses how multiple systems of discrimination compound to affect individuals differently based on their combination of identities.
Intersectionality: A social construct that recognizes the fluid diversity of identities that a person can hold such as gender, race, class, religion, professional status, marital status, socioeconomic status, and others. Intersectionality acknowledges that individuals experience multiple, interconnected systems of privilege and oppression simultaneously.
Institutional Racism: The ways in which institutional policies and practices create different outcomes and opportunities for different groups based on racial discrimination.
Machine Learning Bias: Unfair prejudice embedded in machine learning algorithms that results from biased training data, flawed model design, or discriminatory implementation practices. Machine learning bias can perpetuate historical inequalities and create new forms of discrimination through automated decision-making systems.
Marginalized: Individuals or groups that have been pushed to the edges of society, excluded from mainstream social, economic, political, and cultural opportunities. Marginalization occurs when people are systematically denied access to resources, decision-making processes, and platforms for participation, often due to their identity, circumstances, or characteristics that differ from dominant social norms.
Microaggressions: The verbal, nonverbal, and environmental slights, snubs, insults, or actions, whether intentional or unintentional, which communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages to target people based solely upon discriminatory belief systems.
Neurodiversity: The concept that there is great diversity in how people's brains are wired and work, and that neurological differences should be valued in the same way we value any other human variation.
Power Dynamics: The ways that power, influence, and authority are distributed and exercised within relationships, organizations, and systems, often reflecting broader social hierarchies and inequalities. Understanding power dynamics is essential for creating equitable environments
7
and ensuring that diversity and inclusion efforts address root causes rather than symptoms.
Privilege: Unearned advantages or benefits given to people based on their membership in certain social groups. Privilege can be invisible to those who have it and creates systemic advantages in access to resources, opportunities, and treatment.
Psychological Safety: A belief that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes. Psychological safety enables authentic participation and innovation.
Refugees: Individuals who have been forced to flee their home countries due to persecution, war, violence, or human rights violations and are unable to return safely. Refugees have crossed international borders and often face legal, economic, and social challenges in their host countries while seeking protection and the opportunity to rebuild their lives.
Responsible AI: The practice of understanding, applying, and advocating for AI systems in ways that prioritize human well-being, respect rights and dignity, address potential harms proactively, and ensure that AI development serves beneficial purposes for society. Responsible AI integrates ethical considerations throughout the AI lifecycle rather than treating ethics as an afterthought.
Restorative Justice: An approach to addressing harm that focuses on healing relationships, community accountability, and addressing root causes rather than punishment alone. Restorative justice emphasizes making amends, preventing future harm, and creating conditions for all parties to thrive through community-centered processes.
Social Justice: A broad term that connotes the practice of allyship and coalition work to promote equality, equity, respect, and the assurance of rights within and between communities and social groups.
Stateless Individuals: People who are not considered nationals by any state under the operation of its law, meaning they have no citizenship or nationality. Stateless individuals often lack access to basic rights and services such as education, healthcare, employment, and freedom of movement, facing significant barriers to participating in society and the economy.
Structural Equity: Identifying and removing obstacles in institutions that prevent equal opportunities for everyone. It recognizes that some groups have historically had advantages while others face ongoing disadvantages.
8
Systemic Oppression: The institutionalized mistreatment and disadvantage of specific groups through laws, policies, practices, and cultural norms that create and maintain inequality. Systemic oppression operates through multiple interconnected systems including education, employment, healthcare, and technology access to advantage some groups while disadvantaging others.
Systemic Racism: The complex interaction of culture, policy, and institutions that holds in place the outcomes we see in our lives. Systemic racism is naming the process of white supremacy.
Tokenism: The practice of including individuals from underrepresented groups in minimal numbers or superficial roles to give the appearance of diversity without providing genuine opportunities for influence, leadership, or meaningful participation. Tokenism often places additional burdens on individuals to represent their entire community while maintaining existing power structures.
Underserved Populations: Communities and groups that have historically faced systemic barriers to accessing resources, opportunities, and services, resulting in limited participation in education, technology, economic advancement, and leadership roles. These populations often experience compound disadvantages due to geographic location, economic circumstances, cultural marginalization, or institutional discrimination.
Global Underserved Populations Include:
• Women and girls, particularly in STEM and technology fields
• Racially and ethnically marginalized communities in their respective regions
• Indigenous and First Nations communities worldwide
• People with disabilities (physical, cognitive, sensory, and invisible disabilities)
• LGBTQ+ individuals, especially in regions with limited legal protections
• Rural and remote communities with limited infrastructure access
• Low-income communities and those experiencing poverty
• Refugees, displaced persons, and stateless individuals
• Religiously marginalized and persecuted faith communities
• Older adults facing age discrimination
• Single parents and caregivers with limited mobility
• Individuals with limited formal education or alternative learning paths
• Communities affected by conflict, natural disasters, or political instability
• Linguistically marginalized communities and speakers of endangered languages
• Neurodivergent individuals (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and other cognitive differences)
Underrepresented Populations: Groups that are present in lower numbers or have less visibility and voice in particular fields, industries, or leadership positions relative to their
9
proportion in the general population. Underrepresentation often results from historical exclusion, systemic barriers, or cultural factors that have limited access to certain opportunities, roles, or platforms.
White Supremacy Culture: The dominant cultural patterns and institutional practices that center whiteness as the norm and maintain advantages for white individuals and communities through both explicit and implicit means. This includes organizational cultures that prioritize individualism, perfectionism, and hierarchical thinking while devaluing collaborative, holistic, and culturally diverse approaches.

Course and Workshop Delivery SOP
Purpose:
• Provide consistent, high-quality delivery of SHE IS AI programs globally.
• Develop programs grounded in local context and cultural assets.
• Ensure every AI project is reviewed against ethical, responsible, and inclusive standards before launch.
• Ensure AI tools are used ethically in creating content for SHE IS AI publications, workshops, and courses.
Scope: Applies to all SHE IS AI projects, products, pilots, and collaborations. Covers all live and digital programs delivered under SHE IS AI, including courses, workshops, curricula, and published content.
Responsibilities:
• Content Creators/Instructors: Apply disclosure and quality standards; complete required training and deliver programs.
• Ethics Review Team: Review project proposals and content for ethics; approve content for publication.
• SHE IS AI Leadership: Oversee scheduling and ongoing program monitoring.
• Admin Team: Collect feedback, compile reports, and manage archives.
Procedure:
Stage 1: Preparation & Training
1. Content Creators/Instructors complete SHE IS AI Education Roadmap and SHE IS AI Ethics Training.
Stage 2: Program Design & Community Research
2. Conduct community needs assessment.
3. Map cultural assets and integrate them.
4. Localize content for language and cultural context.
